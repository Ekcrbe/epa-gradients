Methodology

The metric — percentile displacement

With F_global the worldwide CDF of team strength and Q_region the region’s quantile function, on a within-region percentile axis q (a team’s standing among its region’s teams):

D(q) = F_global( Q_region(q) ) − q

D(q) > 0 means a team at the q-th percentile of its region ranks higher than that worldwide — the region is locally harder (deeper). D(q) < 0 is locally easier. Where the curve crosses zero, a region flips from easier to harder (or vice versa) — the shape of each curve is left for the plot itself rather than reduced to a single number. A worldwide-percentile variant, p − F_region(Q_global(p)), is retained in the data.

Team strength

Each snapshot is a postseason (2008–2026): the competitive state after that season. A region’s teams are those that competed in that season — so the count reflects the region’s current depth, not long-defunct teams. Strength is Statbotics’ unitless EPA (an Elo-style, cross-season-comparable rating, ~1500 average). By default each team’s strength is a linear-recency weighted moving average (weights 4:3:2:1) of its unitless EPA over the four most recent seasons up to and including that one, which smooths a living team’s rating; the Single-year EPA toggle uses that season’s EPA alone. The canceled 2020 and 2021 seasons are dropped from every window (and produce no snapshot).

Regions

Present-day boundaries are applied to every season back to 2008. Districts are mapped by team state (multi-state districts such as New England, Chesapeake, and Mid-Atlantic included). Two exceptions are encoded explicitly: South Carolina (its own region through 2022, Peachtree in 2023–24, FIRST South Carolina from 2025) and Pennsylvania (split between Mid-Atlantic and non-district). Non-district teams are grouped by US state, with Québec and Ontario as their own regions and other countries grouped by country.

Baseline & uncertainty

The worldwide baseline is the distribution of all teams for that season (or pooled across all seasons), including the region itself. The shaded band is a 95% bootstrap confidence interval (the region resampled against a fixed world). Small regions are noisy: those with fewer than five teams show no band, and n is shown everywhere — read small samples with the band and count in mind.

Depth ratio & average difficulty

R(x) = (1 − F_region(x)) / (1 − F_global(x)) compares a region’s share of teams above each skill level to the world’s, on a log axis over the global 1st–99th percentile (the 100th is skipped to avoid dividing by zero). Above 1 = deeper than average at that level. The plain mean of R across that same range is each region’s single average difficulty number — the most meaningful one-number summary, and what sorts the All Regions views in the Depth ratio section. The All Regions views in the Displacement section sort by mean D instead, so each section ranks regions by its own metric.

Slope of difficulty

The depth ratio counts the teams above a cutoff but says nothing about how far above they sit. Let e(x) = E[X − x | X > x] — the mean excess, the average gap between a cutoff x and the teams that clear it. Then:

S(x) = e_region(x) / e_global(x)

S > 1 means the teams ahead of x are further ahead locally than worldwide — a steeper climb, where gaining ground costs more EPA per place. S < 1 means they’re bunched closer in, so a small gain passes more of them. (“Steep” here follows the learning-curve sense, not the calculus one: EPA is the independent variable, so a steep climb is a small d(standing)/d(EPA).) Because it divides one gap by another, S is independent of the head count — it is the piece of the picture R cannot see.

The weighted form multiplies by the survival ratio. Since ∫x∞(1−F) = (1−F(x))·e(x), the product S(x)·R(x) is exactly the ratio of the areas under the two survival curves to the right of x — equivalently E[(X−x)+], the total excess strength above x per team. It folds count and magnitude into one number, at the cost of no longer being independent of R. Both forms are averaged over the same 1st–99th global-percentile window as mean R; the mean of the product is taken after multiplying, so it is not the product of the means.

Where a region has no teams above the cutoff there is no gap to measure, so S is undefined rather than zero — those points are gaps in the curve and blank cells in the heatmap.
